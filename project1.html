01 — Problems

Why People Abandon Budgeting Tools

Managing finances often feels like a chore because traditional tools are either too clinical and boring or too complex to navigate. Many users suffer from 'financial fog' — they know they are spending money, but they don't have a clear, real-time visualization of where it's going or how it affects their future goals.

01

Manual Tracking Fatigue

Users give up when adding expenses feels like data entry. The friction of logging each transaction manually leads to abandoned habits within days.

02

Lack of Motivation

Boring, clinical interfaces make users want to avoid looking at their bank balance. When the app feels punishing, people disengage emotionally and practically.

03

Disconnected Data

Information about trips, daily expenses, and team trends are often scattered across different apps — no single source of financial truth.

02 — Ideation & Research

Understanding the User

Before designing anything, I needed to know: why do people abandon budgeting tools? I conducted interviews with working professionals aged 22–35 who manage personal or team finances, uncovering the emotional — not just functional — barriers to engagement.

I open the app, get overwhelmed by numbers, and close it. — User, 27, Marketing Manager
I only check my spending when something goes wrong. — User, 31, Product Designer
I'd love one screen that just tells me what I need to do today. — User, 24, Freelancer
Every time I try to log a trip, I give up halfway — too many steps. — User, 29, Sales Executive

This research helped me gain better insights into the app, understand how people want to interact with it, and identify the exact changes needed to make it genuinely useful rather than performatively functional.

03 — Wireframes

Version 1 — Initial Concept

My initial idea was to create a notification section where users could see system alerts, subscription reminders, and helpful financial tips as soon as they logged in. I wanted the dashboard to feel like an intelligent assistant that talked to the user.

The First Draft

Version 1 featured a sidebar navigation, pending tools panel with counts, recent expenses table, and a notification feed at the bottom. The structure was logical but passive — it showed information without guiding action.

After user walkthroughs, two critical problems emerged that fundamentally undermined the design's purpose.

  • 1Time on a Task: Users frequently accessed specific features yet wasted time searching through sidebar navigation — the home screen provided no direct path to action.
  • 2Cognitive Overload: The notifications section was text-heavy, forcing users to scan and process multiple sentences — increasing stress rather than reducing it.

Version 2 — The Command Center

To solve these issues, I added a Quick Access section, moving the most frequent user actions — New Expense, New Receipt, and New Trip — to the center of the dashboard. This reduced the average number of clicks required to log data by over 50%, making the budgeting process feel seamless and stress-free.

What Changed

The Quick Access bar moved the three most-used actions to the visual center of the dashboard. Notifications were replaced with scannable numbers. The monthly report section gave users instant financial context without requiring navigation.

Design decision: Users were logging expenses 3–5 times per session but spending 30+ seconds navigating each time. Moving Quick Access to the center reduced that to a single tap.
04 — Solution

A Dashboard That Works for You

Feature 01

The Command Center

The home screen was transformed from a passive notification feed into an active command center. Pending approvals, unreported expenses, and recent transactions are visible immediately — as scannable numbers, not paragraphs. The Quick Access bar puts New Expense, New Receipt, and New Trip front and center, cutting navigation time in half.

Feature 02

Calendar Meets Transactions

The Features screen solves the disconnected data problem. A calendar view shows when spending happened, while the live Transactions panel shows what was spent — side by side. Income and Spending totals give users an instant financial health check without digging through reports.

Feature 03

Business Travel, Finally Organised

Trip expenses that used to live across emails, spreadsheets, and receipts are now centralized in one view. Each trip shows destination, budget, report, and approval status — so users know instantly what needs action and what's cleared.

Feature 04

Status Badges that Reduce Anxiety

The Pending vs Approved system removed the anxiety of manually chasing approval confirmations — a pain point mentioned by 4 out of 5 interviewed users. Clear visual states replace ambiguous text-based updates.
