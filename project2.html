01 — Problems

The Hidden Cost of Waiting in Line

Campus breaks are short. But food outlets aren't. Students at our college consistently lose 20–40 minutes of every break standing in queues — time that could be spent studying, resting, or connecting with peers. The problem isn't hunger. It's the system around fulfilling it.

01

Queue Congestion

Peak hours (lunch, post-class breaks) create bottlenecks at every outlet. Students queue for up to 35 minutes just to place an order — before food is even prepared.

02

No Menu Visibility

Students have no way to browse menus or check item availability before arriving. They often reach an outlet only to find their desired item sold out, wasting the entire trip.

03

Outlet Status Unknown

There is no way to know if an outlet is open, busy, or temporarily closed. Students discover this only after physically visiting — compounding frustration during tight breaks.

04

Payment Friction

Cash-only counters slow everything down. Change management, failed UPI transactions at crowded points, and lack of digital records create a chaotic transaction experience.

05

Order Tracking Void

Once an order is placed verbally, students have no confirmation, no estimated time, and no way to know when to collect their food — leading to repeated physical check-ins.

06

Disrupted Schedules

The unpredictability of the dining experience bleeds into class schedules. Students skip meals or arrive late to class rather than risk the queue — a systemic quality-of-life failure.

02 — Ideation & Research

Understanding the User

Before putting pencil to wireframe, I needed to understand the emotional experience of the campus dining problem — not just the logistics. I conducted structured interviews with 18 students across different years and departments, focusing on their daily dining behaviour, frustrations, and ideal scenarios.

I literally skip lunch on busy days. Waiting 30 minutes for a meal I'll eat in 5 isn't worth it. — Student, 2nd Year, Engineering
I wish I could just order from my phone and walk in when it's ready. Like Swiggy, but for college. — Student, 3rd Year, Commerce
Half the time I reach the outlet and they've run out of what I wanted. That's so demoralising. — Student, 1st Year, Science
I have 20 minutes between lectures. By the time I queue and pay, I'm already late for the next one. — Student, 4th Year, Engineering

These conversations surfaced three clear insight clusters that became the foundation for every design decision in QuickBite:

⏱ Time is the core currency — not price
Students want predictability, not just speed
Mobile-first mental model from food delivery apps
Real-time outlet status is critical
Digital confirmation creates psychological relief
03 — Wireframes

Version 1 — Initial Concept

My first wireframe approached QuickBite as a simple directory — a search bar with a grid of outlet cards. Clean, but fundamentally passive. The student still had to do all the work.

The First Draft

The initial concept was a minimalist outlet browser: a search bar at the top, a grid of outlet cards below. Each card showed the outlet name, a food image, and basic info. Simple navigation, no friction on the surface.

But after user walkthroughs, it became clear the interface was solving the wrong problem. It helped users find outlets, but did nothing to help them save time — the core pain point.

  • 1No Real-time Status: Cards showed no indication of whether an outlet was open, busy, or closed. Students would still navigate blind.
  • 2No Ordering Flow: The wireframe stopped at browsing. There was no path from discovery to order — the most critical user journey was completely missing.
  • 3No Context or Filtering: Students couldn't filter by food type, preparation time, or price range. The grid became overwhelming with 6+ outlets.

Version 2 — Redesigned for Action

To fix the core issues, I redesigned around three principles: status visibility, contextual filtering, and a direct path to ordering. The second version introduced a full sidebar navigation, live outlet status indicators, category filters, and metadata (rating, time, price tier) on each card.

What Changed — and Why

Version 2 restructured the entire information hierarchy. The sidebar gave students a persistent navigation context. Live status dots (green = accepting orders, red = busy) solved the visibility problem immediately without requiring any extra interaction.

Category filters let students narrow to their craving in one tap. Rating, prep time, and price tier on each card gave them the decision-making context they needed before clicking through — reducing exploration time dramatically.

Design decision: The "Busy — Notify Me" state for unavailable outlets turned a dead end into a useful touchpoint. Students could opt-in to a notification and return to browsing rather than feeling blocked.
04 — Solution

A Platform That Works Around Your Schedule

The final QuickBite experience is built around one principle: every second a student spends navigating the app is a second stolen from their break. Every screen was designed to get out of the way as fast as possible.

Feature 01

Live Outlet Status

Every outlet card shows a real-time status dot — green means accepting orders, red means busy. Students know before clicking whether an outlet can serve them. A "Notify Me" state converts a dead-end into a re-engagement touchpoint.

Why it matters: In user testing, this single feature reduced "wasted visits" — students walking to a closed outlet — by eliminating the core information gap.
Feature 02

Browse & Order Flow

Selecting an outlet opens a full menu view with item availability, customisation options, and a persistent cart. Students can browse across multiple outlets and consolidate orders before checkout — eliminating multi-queue scenarios.

Design decision: The menu screen mirrors familiar food delivery patterns (Swiggy/Zomato) so there's zero learning curve — students are already fluent in this interaction language.
Feature 03

Order Tracking & Ready Alerts

After placing an order, students receive a live tracker with estimated prep time and a push notification when their order is ready for pickup. They stay in class or elsewhere until their exact window arrives.

Impact: This is the core time-saving mechanism — students collect food in under 2 minutes by arriving precisely when it's ready, not before.
Feature 04

Wallet & Seamless Payments

A built-in wallet allows students to preload credits and pay in a single tap. No fumbling for cash, no UPI delays at the counter. Order history provides digital receipts and spending patterns across outlets.

Why it matters: Payment friction was the second-biggest reported blocker after queues. Digital pre-payment removes the counter interaction entirely — the student simply picks up and walks away.
